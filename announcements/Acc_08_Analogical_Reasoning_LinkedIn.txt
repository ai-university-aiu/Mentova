A knife is to cutting as a hammer is to — what?

This is not a trivia question. It is a test of whether a mind can recognise that two objects share the same relational structure, then transfer that structure to a new case.

A knife and a hammer look nothing alike. But they are connected to their functions by the same type of relation: capable_of. Once you see that, you can complete the analogy.

Mentova sees it:

  answer(drive_nail, just(analogy(knife, cut, hammer, drive_nail, via([capable_of]))))

knife capable_of cut :: hammer capable_of drive_nail. Filler: drive_nail. Relation: capable_of. Named. Justified. Auditable.

Try a taxonomic analogy:

  answer(bird, just(analogy(canary, bird, eagle, bird, via([is_a]))))

canary is_a bird :: eagle is_a bird. Both are birds. The shared relation is is_a. The filler is bird.

Try a causal analogy:

  answer(smoke, just(analogy(rain, wet_ground, fire, smoke, via([causes]))))

rain causes wet_ground :: fire causes smoke. The causal structure maps from one domain (weather) to another (fire). No additional knowledge needed — the relational pattern transfers.

Try a taxonomic analogy at a different level:

  answer(tree, just(analogy(rose, flower, oak, tree, via([is_a]))))

rose is_a flower :: oak is_a tree. Both are instances of their natural kind.

The method is structure mapping: find the relation R such that R(A, B) holds, then find D such that R(C, D) holds. The justification names the relation type and the source pair, so you can audit the transfer.

This is Gentner's structure-mapping theory (1983) implemented in glass-box Prolog. Not a black-box embedding similarity. A named relational transfer with a readable justification.

Four analogies. Four correct fillers. Four relation types demonstrated: is_a, capable_of, causes — the relational fabric of the Small-World knowledge base.
