Mentova now searches with heuristics. Rung 29 of 48 is accomplished.

Finding a path through a graph with five nodes and eight edges is a search problem. Trying every possible path is brute force. Using a heuristic — an estimate of how far you are from the goal — is intelligence.

Rung 29 gives Mentova heuristic-guided search.

A five-node graph: a, b, c, d, e. Edges with costs. Heuristic estimates of distance remaining to e.

Greedy best-first search:

  Query: greedy(a, e, 10)
  Answer: path([a, c, e], cost(10))
  Justification: greedy_search(a, e, path([a,c,e]), cost(10))

At node a: two neighbors — b (h=6) and c (h=4). Pick c. From c: two neighbors — d (h=2) and e (h=0). Pick e. Done.

Path: [a, c, e]. Cost: 10.

The heuristic guided the search. No wasted exploration. No backtracking needed.

Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm confirms: optimal cost from a to e is also 10. The greedy heuristic was good enough to find the optimal path here.

  Query: shortest(a, e)
  Answer: path([a,c,e], cost(10))
  Justification: dijkstra(a, e, path([a,c,e]), cost(10))

Greedy is fast. Dijkstra is optimal. Both are available. Both give justified answers.

Heuristic reasoning is what GPS uses to route you around traffic. What logistics software uses to allocate trucks. What game AI uses to navigate a character through a dungeon.

The difference between brute-force search and heuristic search is the difference between trying every possible route and knowing which direction feels closer to the destination.

Mentova now has that sense of direction.
