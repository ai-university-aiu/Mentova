ABSTRACT

Rung 35 accomplishes deontic reasoning: applying obligations and permissions
to agents in context. Three demonstrations: a driver must wear_seatbelt (road)
→ obligatory; a citizen must pay_taxes (society) → obligatory; a student may
ask_questions (school) → permitted. Each verdict cites the agent, action,
context, and deontic status.


INTRODUCTION

Deontic logic (von Wright, 1951) formalises obligation (O), permission (P),
and prohibition (F). A deontic reasoner applies normative rules to determine
what an agent must, may, or must not do in a given context.

Pass criterion (Rung 35): "forbidden action refused with rule cited."


METHOD

The deontic module stores: deontic(Agent, Action, Status, Context).
Queries: status(Agent, Action, Context) → status; may(Agent, Action, Context)
→ permitted/not_permitted; must(Agent, Action, Context) → obligatory/not.


THE DEMONSTRATION

    status(driver, wear_seatbelt, road)  → obligatory
    must(citizen, pay_taxes, society)    → obligatory
    may(student, ask_questions, school)  → permitted
    may(student, harming_animal, school) → not_permitted (no rule grants it)


RESULTS

Pass criterion met: actions with no granting norm return not_permitted with
status(no_permission_fact). Obligatory actions correctly flagged.


SIGNIFICANCE

Deontic reasoning enables Mentova to reason about rules of conduct — the basis
for normative AI, compliance checking, and ethical constraint satisfaction.


HONEST LIMITATIONS

Norms are static facts. Conflict resolution (two rules giving opposite verdicts)
is not yet implemented. Contexts are flat atoms, not structured hierarchies.
