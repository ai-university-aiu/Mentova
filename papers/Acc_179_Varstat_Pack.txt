Abstract

PrologAI gains its 159th work package: the varstat pack (vt_*, Layer 138).

The varstat pack provides 14 predicates for integer summation, floor and
rounded arithmetic means, per-element signed and absolute deviation from the
floor mean, per-row and per-column sums and means, global grid mean, and
sorted position lists for cells above or below various means in 2D integer
grids.

Introduction

Statistical summary measures are among the most universal analytical tools
in data analysis.

For 2D integer grids of the kind used in visual reasoning benchmarks, three
classes of statistics are especially useful.

The first class is sums: the total of all values in a list, in each row,
in each column, or across the entire grid.

The second class is means: the arithmetic average, computed as sum divided
by count. For integer arithmetic, this yields a floor mean (truncated toward
zero) or a rounded mean (nearest integer with banker's rounding).

The third class is deviations: how much each value differs from the mean.
A signed deviation is positive when the value exceeds the mean and negative
when it falls short. An absolute deviation ignores the sign.

These three classes combine naturally for answering questions such as: which
cells are above the average for their row? Which cells exceed the global
mean? What is the spread of values around the mean in each column?

Predicates

The 14 public predicates are:

vt_sum(+List, -Sum)

Sum is the integer total of all values in List.

vt_mean_floor(+List, -Mean)

Mean is the floor of the arithmetic mean: sum(List) // length(List).
Fails when List is empty.

vt_mean_round(+List, -Mean)

Mean is the rounded arithmetic mean using float division followed by
banker's rounding (round/1 in SWI-Prolog, which rounds half to even).
Fails when List is empty.

vt_deviation(+List, -Devs)

Devs[I] is the signed difference between List[I] and the floor mean.
Negative values are below the mean; positive values are above.

vt_abs_deviation(+List, -AbsDevs)

AbsDevs[I] is the absolute value of the deviation: |List[I] - floor_mean|.
Always non-negative.

vt_row_sums(+Grid, -Sums)

Sums[R] is the integer sum of all values in row R of Grid.

vt_col_sums(+Grid, -Sums)

Sums[C] is the integer sum of all values in column C of Grid.

vt_row_means(+Grid, -Means)

Means[R] is the floor mean of all values in row R.

vt_col_means(+Grid, -Means)

Means[C] is the floor mean of all values in column C.

vt_global_mean(+Grid, -Mean)

Mean is the floor mean of all cell values across the entire grid.

vt_above_mean(+Grid, -Cells)

Cells is the sorted list of R-C positions whose value is strictly greater
than the global floor mean. Enumerated in row-major order.

vt_below_mean(+Grid, -Cells)

Cells is the sorted list of R-C positions whose value is strictly less than
the global floor mean.

vt_row_above_mean(+Grid, -Cells)

Cells is the sorted list of R-C positions whose value is strictly greater
than the floor mean of their own row.

vt_col_above_mean(+Grid, -Cells)

Cells is the sorted list of R-C positions whose value is strictly greater
than the floor mean of their own column. Enumerated in row-major order.

Example Results

For G = [[1,2,3],[4,5,6],[7,8,9]]:

vt_sum([1,2,3], S) gives S = 6.

vt_mean_floor([1,2,3], M) gives M = 2.

vt_row_sums(G, S) gives S = [6, 15, 24]: row 0 sums to 6, row 1 to 15,
row 2 to 24.

vt_col_sums(G, S) gives S = [12, 15, 18]: column 0 sums to 12, column 1
to 15, column 2 to 18.

vt_row_means(G, M) gives M = [2, 5, 8]: each row has 3 values, floors are
2, 5, 8.

vt_col_means(G, M) gives M = [4, 5, 6]: column means are 12//3=4, 15//3=5,
18//3=6.

vt_global_mean(G, M) gives M = 5: total is 45, divided by 9 cells = 5.

vt_above_mean(G, C) gives C = [1-2, 2-0, 2-1, 2-2]: the cells with values
6, 7, 8, 9 are all above the global mean of 5.

vt_row_above_mean(G, C) gives C = [0-2, 1-2, 2-2]: in each row, only the
rightmost cell (3, 6, 9) exceeds its row mean (2, 5, 8).

vt_col_above_mean(G, C) gives C = [2-0, 2-1, 2-2]: in each column, only
the bottom cell (7, 8, 9) exceeds its column mean (4, 5, 6).

Implementation

vt_mean_floor uses integer floor division: Mean is Sum // N.

vt_mean_round uses floating-point division: Mean is round(float(Sum) / float(N)).
The SWI-Prolog round/1 function applies banker's rounding (round half to even):
round(1.5) = 2, round(2.5) = 2, round(3.5) = 4.

vt_col_above_mean enumerates in row-major order (R outer, C inner). For each
(R, C) pair, it re-computes the column mean via a nested findall over all rows.
This is O(H * W * H) but simple and deterministic.

The sum_list/2 predicate from library(lists) is used throughout for efficient
summation.
