Task 36a08778 shows grids with a few 6-coloured seed cells at the top. Everything else is background (7) or obstacle rows (2s). The output traces 6-filled paths from the seeds downward and around the obstacles - exactly like water flowing under gravity.

The glass-box rule I call waterfall:

Seeds are the existing 6-cells. The BFS runs row-major (top-first priority). Each 6-cell either (a) flows straight down into background, or (b) if blocked by an in-grid obstacle, spreads horizontally left and right in its current row. Spreading stops at the first cell that CAN fall (drain point, included) or at a barrier (existing 6, obstacle, boundary). Critically: hitting the bottom of the grid does NOT trigger spreading - water simply stops there.

The result is a connected network of 6-paths that outlines every obstacle cluster and links back to the seeds. All 6 training pairs (28 to 65 changes each, grids from 8x9 to 15x16) and the test case verify correctly in both Python and SWI-Prolog.
