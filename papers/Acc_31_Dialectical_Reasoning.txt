ABSTRACT

We report Rung 31 of Mentova's 48-rung reasoning ladder: dialectical reasoning.
Mentova weighs pro-arguments against con-arguments for a contested proposition
and synthesises a verdict. For the proposition "nuclear energy is good," six
arguments total (three pro, three con) are weighted; pro side wins with total
weight 2.4 vs 2.0, and the decisive argument is named: low_carbon_emission.
Pass criterion met: contested proposition decided with weights and synthesis.


INTRODUCTION

Dialectical reasoning models the structure of debate: for every claim, there are
supporting and opposing arguments of varying strength. The stronger side prevails.
Rung 31 equips Mentova with an argument base and a weighted voting mechanism.


BACKGROUND

Dialectical reasoning is studied in informal logic (Walton, 1989) and computational
argumentation (Dung, 1995). This implementation uses additive weight scoring
without attack/support graph structure.


METHOD

A new module, src/mentova/dialectical.pl, implements:

    mentova_dialectical(+Query, -Result, -Justification)

For three propositions (nuclear_energy_good, remote_work_better, ai_dangerous),
pro and con arguments with weights are stored. The debate/1 query aggregates
scores and names the winning synthesis argument.


DEMONSTRATION

    ?- mentova_query(dialectical, debate(nuclear_energy_good), R).
    R = answer(verdict(pro, synthesis(low_carbon_emission)),
               just(dialectical(proposition(nuclear_energy_good),
                                 pro_score(2.4), con_score(2.0),
                                 winner(pro),
                                 synthesis(low_carbon_emission))))


RESULTS

Pro side wins 2.4 to 2.0. Decisive argument: low_carbon_emission.
Pass criterion met.


SIGNIFICANCE

Dialectical reasoning is the foundation of debate, negotiation, and policy
analysis. A system that can weigh both sides and identify the strongest
argument is more trustworthy than one that accepts claims without counter-evaluation.


HONEST LIMITATIONS

The scoring is additive and unweighted by source credibility. A full
argumentation framework would include attack/support links and extensions.
