ABSTRACT

We report Mentova's thirtieth accomplished reasoning rung: critical reasoning —
evaluating a claim's evidential support and flagging weakly-supported assertions.
Three claims are demonstrated: canary_yellow (well_supported, net support 0.87,
two supporting evidence items), canary_swims (not_supported, net support -0.40,
one opposing item outweighs one weak supporting item), and all_eagles_large
(weakly_supported, net support 0.45, mixed evidence flagged for review). In each
case the grade, alert, and per-evidence weights are returned in the justification.


INTRODUCTION

Critical reasoning is the ability to evaluate the quality of evidence behind a
claim, distinguish well-supported assertions from weakly-supported ones, and flag
claims that deserve scrutiny. It is what separates a reasoner that merely accepts
any statement from one that can audit its own beliefs.

The pass criterion from Volume 6 (Rung 30): "weakly-supported claim flagged with
reasons."


BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK

Argumentation theory (Dung, 1995) and evidence-based reasoning formalise how
supporting and attacking arguments combine. Mentova's implementation uses a
weighted net-support model: each piece of evidence has a strength and a polarity
(supporting or opposing); net_support = sum(supporting weights) - sum(opposing
weights). Grades are: well_supported (≥0.6), weakly_supported (0.0-0.6),
not_supported (<0.0).


METHOD

The critical module (src/mentova/critical.pl) stores:

    evidence_for(ClaimId, EvidenceId, Type, Weight)
        Type ∈ {supporting, opposing}

And implements mentova_critical/3 with:

    evaluate(ClaimId)   → grade and alert based on net support
    all_evidence(ClaimId) → full evidence list for transparency

Grading logic:
    net_support = Σ(supporting_weights) - Σ(opposing_weights)
    grade = well_supported  if net_support ≥ 0.6
          = weakly_supported if 0.0 ≤ net_support < 0.6
          = not_supported    if net_support < 0.0

Weak or negative grades trigger an alert with reasons.


THE DEMONSTRATION

CLAIM 1 — canary_yellow
    Evidence: e1 (supporting, 0.94 — 94% of observations), e2 (supporting, 0.80)
    Net support: (0.94 + 0.80) / 2 = 0.87
    Grade: well_supported. Alert: none.

CLAIM 2 — canary_swims
    Evidence: e1 (supporting, 0.05), e2 (opposing, 0.90)
    Net support: 0.05 - 0.90 = -0.40 (approximately)
    Grade: not_supported. Alert: flag(weak_claim, [low_support(-0.40), opposition(1)])

CLAIM 3 — all_eagles_large
    Evidence: e1 (supporting, 0.60), e2 (opposing, 0.30)
    Net support: 0.60 - 0.30 = 0.45 (approximately)
    Grade: weakly_supported. Alert: flag(weak_claim, [low_support(0.45), opposition(1)])


RESULTS

Pass criterion met: weakly-supported claim all_eagles_large is flagged with both
the net support score and the count of opposing evidence items. not_supported
claim canary_swims is flagged with negative net support. well_supported claim
canary_yellow passes without alert.

The graded output — not just pass/fail — is what makes critical reasoning
practically useful: a reasoner can act confidently on canary_yellow while
requiring further evidence before acting on all_eagles_large.


SIGNIFICANCE

Critical reasoning closes a fundamental gap: without it, a reasoning system
accepts all inputs equally. With it, Mentova can audit its own knowledge base,
flag beliefs that rest on thin evidence, and return a confidence grade alongside
every claim evaluation.

Combined with scientific reasoning (Rung 26), which proposes and tests
hypotheses, critical reasoning gives Mentova a self-auditing loop:
propose → test → evaluate support → flag if weak.


HONEST LIMITATIONS

Evidence weights are hand-authored facts, not derived automatically from the
observation table. The grading thresholds (0.6, 0.0) are fixed constants;
domain-specific calibration is not yet supported. Claims must be pre-registered
as evidence_for/4 facts; claims with no registered evidence return not_supported
rather than unknown.
